  • A:
    This is a sequential color scale (without a middle). I do think this is an effective color scale. This is because hue and saturation covary, making differences very easy to spot (because multiple channels can be referenced).

  • B:
    This is not an effective color scale for this task. This is because the middle is not neutral, and rather appears to be a third peak. This may lead to confusion from users on what the neutral middleground actually indicates.

  • C:
    Using the pilestone.com simulator, it is evident that the scale is subjected to the following effects under various colorblindness anomalies:

    Deuteranomaly:
    Hues are different (duller) but hue variation is still visible

    Deuteranopia:
    Hues are different (blue-red tinted) but hue variation is still visible

    Protanomaly:
    Hues are different (duller) but hue variation is still visible

    Protanopia:
    Hues are different (purple-yellow tinted) but hue variation is still visible

    Tritanomaly:
    Hues variation is difficult to detect (but still slightly visible) -- this scale appears to vary more in terms of luminance and saturation

    Tritanopia:
    Hues variation is very difficult to detect -- this scale appears to vary in terms of luminance and saturation

    Monochromacy / Achromatopsia:
    Hues variation is not detectable -- this scale appears to vary in terms of saturation

    Blue cone monochromacy:
    Hues variation is difficult to detect (but still slightly visible) -- this scale appears to be duller, and to vary more in terms of luminance

  • D:
    I would not recommend that the data scientist use this scale. Firstly, the scale is not perceptually uniform -- there appear to be luminosity peaks in the cyan and yellow regions, which could be confusing as those are not representative of actual peaks in the data. Second, a rainbow scale is an especially poor choice for representing ordinal, ratio, or interval data, because varying hue repeatedly (over a lot of hues) is not helpful for indicating greater or lesser values, such as average life expectancies; without referencing a key, users will not have any indicators for how to compare values to each other.
